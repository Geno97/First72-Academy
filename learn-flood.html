Sandbagging & Flood Response

Protect people and property from flooding over three days — understanding flood risk, building effective barriers, and managing water safely when it gets through.

Understand flood risk

Floods are New Zealand's most frequent natural hazard. Before you sandbag anything, you need to understand how water behaves and when it becomes deadly.

Video lesson · coming soon

A short introduction to how floods develop, how fast water can rise, and why even shallow water is dangerous.

How floods happen

Floods come in several forms, and each behaves differently. Understanding which type you're facing shapes how you respond.

  • Surface flooding — heavy rain overwhelms drains and pools on the ground. Common in towns and cities, and often fast.
  • River (fluvial) flooding — rivers swell and overtop their banks. Often slower to build but can last for days.
  • Flash flooding — intense rain in a short time, often in hilly areas. Extremely dangerous because of how fast it arrives.
  • Coastal flooding — storm surge and high tides push seawater inland.

In a changing climate, severe rainfall events are becoming more common in New Zealand. The Auckland Anniversary floods and Cyclone Gabrielle in 2023 showed how quickly ordinary areas can become dangerous.

Why water is so dangerous

People consistently underestimate moving water. The numbers are sobering:

  • Just 15cm of fast-flowing water can knock an adult off their feet.
  • 30cm of water can float many cars; 60cm can sweep most vehicles away.
  • Floodwater is rarely clean — it carries sewage, chemicals, and debris you can't see.
  • You cannot tell the depth or current of brown floodwater by looking. What seems shallow may hide a washed-out road or open drain.
Example
During Cyclone Gabrielle, many people were caught out not by deep water but by how fast ordinary streams became raging torrents — sometimes in under an hour. The lesson responders repeat: if you can't see the bottom, don't enter it.
Never underestimate moving water. Even shallow, flowing water can knock you down and sweep you away.

Reading your own flood risk

Knowing your specific risk lets you prepare before water arrives. Check what applies to you:

  • Is your property near a river, stream, or the coast?
  • Have there been floods in your area before? Local councils often hold flood maps.
  • Where would water enter your building first — low doors, garages, airbricks?
  • What's your safe higher ground, and how would you get there?

Sandbagging & barriers

Done right, sandbags protect homes and buildings. Done wrong, they offer false comfort. Today is the practical craft of building an effective barrier.

⏱ 35 min📚 4 sections✅ Activity + quiz
Video lesson · coming soon

A practical demonstration of filling, placing, and stacking sandbags to build a barrier that holds.

What sandbags can and can't do

It's important to be realistic. Sandbags slow and divert water and reduce how much gets in — they rarely make a building completely watertight. Their job is to buy time and reduce damage, especially around the points where water enters first.

Sandbags divert and slow water; they don't make a building waterproof. Use them to reduce and delay flooding, not to stop it entirely.

Filling sandbags correctly

How you fill a bag matters as much as where you place it.

  • Fill bags about one-half to two-thirds full — never packed tight. A partly-filled bag moulds to the ground and to other bags, sealing gaps.
  • Sand is ideal, but soil or a sand/soil mix works.
  • Fold or tie the open end closed, and lay that end facing away from the water flow.
  • Don't overfill — a rock-hard bag won't settle into place and leaves gaps water pours through.

Building the barrier

Lay sandbags like brickwork — the same principle that makes a brick wall strong applies here.

  • Clear the area first so bags sit on a clean surface.
  • Lay the first row lengthways, end to end, in the direction of the flow.
  • Stack the next row offset like bricks, so joints don't line up (lining them up creates channels for water).
  • Tamp each bag down firmly to seal gaps and lock the layers together.
  • Protect doorways, garage doors, airbricks, and low windows first — these are the usual entry points.
Example
A homeowner spent an hour sandbagging their front door, only for water to pour in through the garage and a low airbrick they'd ignored. Lesson: defend every low entry point, not just the obvious one.

Alternatives and add-ons

Sandbags aren't the only option, and a few extras improve them:

  • Plastic sheeting laid against the building behind sandbags greatly improves the seal.
  • Purpose-made flood barriers and water-filled tubes are faster than sandbags if you have them.
  • Raising valuables and unplugging electrics before water arrives limits the damage that gets through.

Pumps, safety & recovery

How to use pumps safely around water and electricity, and the key safety rules for flood response.

Using pumps to clear water

When water gets in, pumps help clear it — but they must be used carefully, especially because of the deadly combination of water and electricity.

  • Keep all electrical equipment, cords, and connections out of the water.
  • Use a pump rated for the job, and follow its instructions.
  • Direct pumped water well away from the building so it doesn't simply flow back.
  • Don't pump out a flooded basement too fast while water outside is still high — the pressure difference can damage walls. Lower the level gradually.

The safety rules that matter most

Flood response causes injuries and deaths every year, almost always because people take risks with water. These rules are non-negotiable:

  • Never enter floodwater unless you absolutely must — and never if it's moving.
  • Never drive through floodwater. Most flood deaths in vehicles happen in water the driver thought was shallow.
  • Treat water and electricity as deadly together. If water is near power outlets or your switchboard, turn off the power at the mains if safe to do so, and never touch electrics with wet hands or while standing in water.
  • Never work alone in or near rising water.
  • Know when to stop. Property can be repaired or replaced; people can't.
If water is rising faster than you can manage it, stop and get to safety. No possession is worth your life.

After the flood

Recovery has its own hazards. Floodwater leaves behind contamination and damage that isn't always obvious.

  • Assume floodwater and anything it touched is contaminated — wear gloves and wash thoroughly.
  • Don't turn power back on until an electrician has checked a flood-affected building.
  • Photograph all damage before you clean up, for insurance.
  • Throw out food, medicine, and drinking water that floodwater has contacted.
  • Watch for stress and fatigue — recovery is exhausting, and looking after yourself matters.
Example
After a flood, a family rushed to switch the power back on to start cleaning up. The wet switchboard caused a dangerous fault. An electrician's check first — frustrating as the wait was — would have prevented it.
