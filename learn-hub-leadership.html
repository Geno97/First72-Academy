Leadership for Community Hub Teams

Leading in the first 72 hours using the NZ Community Emergency Hub model — grassroots response, shared decisions, local problem solving. Work through the 10 modules below at your own pace.

Click any module to expand it. Each one has a short lesson, and most include an activity or a quick knowledge check. Take your time — there's no rush.

01

What is a Community Emergency Hub?

Start with the shared purpose before talking leadership.

 OPEN / CLOSE

A Community Emergency Hub is a place for the community to come together and help itself when a disaster strikes. It's run by locals, for locals — not by officials.

A hub is a place for the community to:

  • Provide a safe gathering place where people can support one another.
  • Share information so people understand what's happening and how to stay safe.
  • Identify local problems, issues, and strengths.
  • Solve problems using what the community has available locally.
  • Gather local information that can be passed on to the ECC/EOC (Emergency Coordination Centre / Emergency Operations Centre).

Solve problems close to home first

The model works outward in four levels, solving as much as possible at each stage before escalating:

  • 1. Household — make yourself and your home safe first.
  • 2. Neighbours — check on nearby needs and pool help.
  • 3. Community — coordinate local help through the hub.
  • 4. ECC / EOC — escalate the bigger issues that local effort can't solve.
Communities that can support themselves help official agencies prioritise the most urgent call-outs.
Knowledge check
1. What is the main purpose of a Community Emergency Hub?
To replace the Emergency Coordination Centre To help a community gather, share information, and solve local problems together To wait for official instructions before doing anything
2. In the "close to home first" model, what comes before escalating to the ECC/EOC?
Household, neighbours, then community-level problem solving Nothing — escalate everything immediately Only the council can act first
Why this matters

Solving problems locally first frees official responders to focus on the most life-threatening, large-scale needs.

02

Leadership in a Hub

What makes leadership different in a neighbour-led response?

 OPEN / CLOSE

Leadership in a community hub is different from leadership in an official agency. You don't have a rank or a rulebook to fall back on — you have people who've chosen to help.

  • There is no special legal authority or command power for community hub volunteers.
  • The initial hub leader influences and coordinates rather than commands.
  • Major decisions should be discussed and agreed with the wider team.
  • Leaders can shift or change as more volunteers arrive and someone else is collectively agreed on.
You lead through people, not authority.
Reflect

Think of a time you led without authority

Recall a moment you had to get a group moving without being "in charge" — a sports team, a worksite, a community event. What actually made people follow your lead? Note two or three things that worked. Those same behaviours are what hold a hub together.

Knowledge check
1. A community hub leader's authority comes mainly from:
A legal command power granted in emergencies Influence, coordination, and the trust of the team Being the oldest person present
2. Major decisions in a hub should be:
Discussed and agreed with the wider team Made alone and quickly by the first leader, always
03

Role of the Hub Supervisor

A facilitation role that keeps the whole hub functioning.

 OPEN / CLOSE

The Hub Supervisor keeps everything running — not by controlling people, but by making sure the hub stays safe, coordinated, and sustainable. The role covers six areas:

Safety & oversight

Keep the hub running safely and make sure people are not working in isolation.

Role allocation

Help allocate roles, cover gaps, and make sure everyone knows who is doing what.

Team meetings

Bring people together regularly so priorities, decisions, and handovers stay aligned.

Wellbeing & roster

Prompt breaks, hydration, rest, and role changes when stress or fatigue becomes visible.

Records

Keep records of major decisions so later shifts understand what happened and what still needs doing.

Media & closure

Handle media carefully, and know when to close overnight, reduce numbers, or stand the hub down.

Supervisor doesn't mean command-and-control. It means visible coordination, facilitation, and sustainability.
Knowledge check
1. Which of these is a core Hub Supervisor responsibility?
Making sure no one is working in isolation and that fatigue is managed Personally making every decision in the hub Speaking to media without any care for what's shared
2. Why keep records of major decisions?
So later shifts understand what happened and what still needs doing Only for legal reasons after the event
04

Leading a Team: Working as a Group

How to turn willing volunteers into a functioning team quickly.

 OPEN / CLOSE

When people arrive wanting to help, your job is to turn that goodwill into an organised team — fast. Five simple steps:

  • 1. Gather — bring everyone together and agree why you're here.
  • 2. Clarify — set initial objectives, priorities, and safety boundaries.
  • 3. Assign — fill priority roles first, then expand as numbers grow.
  • 4. Connect — keep information flowing across roles and teams.
  • 5. Review — come back together regularly and reset priorities.
Leadership = keeping the team connected.
Knowledge check
1. What should you do first when volunteers arrive?
Gather them and agree why you're all here Send them home until officials arrive Give everyone a different task with no overview
2. "Review" in the five steps means:
Regrouping regularly to reset priorities as things change Writing a report after the emergency is over
05

The First 72 Hours Reality

The environment is messy. Leadership still has to function.

 OPEN / CLOSE

In the first 72 hours after a major event, conditions are difficult and information is thin. Good leaders expect this and lead anyway.

  • Power, water, roads, and communications may be disrupted.
  • Information will often be incomplete, delayed, or conflicting.
  • Demand for help may rise faster than volunteers can organise.
  • Outside support may be focused on higher-priority or larger-scale impacts.
  • Local leaders may have to act while uncertainty is still high.
You must cope with, and manage, uncertainty.
Knowledge check
1. In the first 72 hours, information will usually be:
Incomplete, delayed, or conflicting Complete, accurate, and on time
2. What should a local leader do while uncertainty is still high?
Act on the best verified information available, and keep reassessing Wait until everything is fully confirmed before doing anything
06

Priorities & Decision-Making

A simple hierarchy, and a shared decision cycle.

 OPEN / CLOSE

Priorities for leaders

When everything feels urgent, this order helps teams decide what matters first:

  • 1. Life safety — immediate threats, injuries, rescue needs, and 111 where required.
  • 2. Information — build a usable, verified picture of what's happening.
  • 3. Welfare — shelter, comfort, accessibility, hygiene, and practical needs.
  • 4. Coordination — match people, tasks, and resources so effort isn't wasted.
Prioritisation drives action.

The shared decision cycle

Shared decisions still need structure. Use this four-step loop:

  • 1. Gather facts — what is confirmed? What is urgent?
  • 2. Discuss as a team — bring the right roles into the decision.
  • 3. Record & assign — write it down and make ownership visible.
  • 4. Review — reassess as new information arrives.
Decisions must be shared and visible.
Scenario · decide as a group

The bridge reports

Three community members report a nearby bridge may be damaged. One says it's impassable. Another says cars are still using it. A third says elderly neighbours on the far side need help. Work through: What is verified right now? What action is justified now? Who needs to be involved? What must be recorded and communicated?

See a strong approach

Right now, very little is verified — reports conflict. Justified action: send a reliable person to assess the bridge safely (don't cross it), and treat it as unsafe until confirmed. Involve: whoever is doing information coordination, plus anyone with local knowledge of the bridge. Record: the conflicting reports, the time, and the decision to verify before acting; communicate "bridge status unconfirmed — avoid" on the public board until you know more.

Knowledge check
1. What is the leader's first priority?
Life safety Coordination Media management
2. In the shared decision cycle, what comes right after gathering facts?
Discuss as a team Stand the hub down
07

Assigning Roles Early

Fill the core roles first. Expand as capacity grows.

 OPEN / CLOSE

Don't try to fill every role at once. Stand up the core functions first, then add more as people arrive.

Priority roles first

  • Hub Supervisor
  • Information Coordination
  • Public Information
  • Reception

Then add as capacity grows

  • Needs and Offers
  • Community Space
  • Welfare / Empathy
  • Communications
  • Building / Facility Maintenance
Knowledge check
1. Which of these is a priority role to fill first?
Hub Supervisor Building / Facility Maintenance
2. How should roles be filled?
Core roles first, then expand as more volunteers arrive All roles at once, immediately, no matter how many people you have
08

Information Leadership

Good information is one of the hub's most valuable outputs.

 OPEN / CLOSE

A hub runs on information. Managing it well is one of the most valuable things you can do.

  • Create and maintain a situation board.
  • Mark information that is not yet verified.
  • Update the public information board clearly and visibly.
  • Send status reports and major impacts or needs to the ECC/EOC.
  • Keep a record of incoming and outgoing messages.
Activity · filter the information

Sort these reports

Before information shapes a decision, sort it into Verified (safe to use), Needs checking (investigate or mark unverified), or Do not use yet (rumour, hearsay, or sensitive). Try these:

  • "Road blocked at Smith St"
  • "Water unsafe in two streets"
  • "Bridge might be damaged"
  • "Evacuated family needs shelter"
  • "Someone heard the hub is closing"
See suggested sorting

"Evacuated family needs shelter" is usually verifiable on the spot → act. "Road blocked" and "water unsafe" need quick checking before going on the public board. "Bridge might be damaged" → needs checking, mark unverified. "Someone heard the hub is closing" is hearsay → do not use yet; don't repeat it publicly until confirmed.

Knowledge check
1. Information that isn't yet confirmed should be:
Clearly marked as unverified, not treated as fact Shared publicly straight away to be safe
2. Who should receive status reports on major impacts and needs?
The ECC / EOC No one — keep it within the hub only
09

Supporting People & LOOK–LISTEN–LINK

Leadership behaviour shapes the emotional climate of the hub.

 OPEN / CLOSE

How you behave sets the tone for everyone. People take their emotional cues from the leader.

Do

  • Listen respectfully.
  • Help people keep control of their own decisions.
  • Link people to information, comfort, and practical support.
  • Prompt breaks, food, water, and rotation for volunteers.

Avoid

  • Ordering people around without explanation.
  • Talking down to people or minimising their feelings.
  • Promising things will be fine when you don't know.
  • Treating anger or distress as a personal attack.

The LOOK – LISTEN – LINK tool

  • LOOK — what is happening? What is verified? What are the risks and unmet needs?
  • LISTEN — what are people telling you? Who has information, skills, needs, or concerns?
  • LINK — connect needs to offers, people to information, and local problems to local solutions.
Role-play · facilitate, don't control

Three pressure moments

Practise staying calm and facilitating (not controlling) in each: (1) a volunteer surge where people arrive wanting to help but don't know what to do; (2) a distressed community member wanting answers you can't fully give yet; (3) two volunteers disagreeing publicly about what's happening. In each case, model the calm behaviour you want the whole hub to copy.

Knowledge check
1. Which is a supportive leadership behaviour?
Listening respectfully and helping people keep control of their own decisions Promising everything will be fine when you don't actually know
2. The "LINK" in LOOK–LISTEN–LINK means:
Connecting needs to offers and people to information Locking down all information until officials arrive
10

Practice Scenarios & Key Takeaways

Bring the whole model together, then lock it in.

 OPEN / CLOSE
Activity · build a hub

You have 5 volunteers and a community hall

Limited information, and community members are starting to arrive. Decide: assign the first roles; choose the first 3 priorities; describe how decisions will be made; identify one major risk and how you'll manage it. Focus on simplicity and function, not perfect detail.

Capstone · final scenario

Earthquake, flood, or tsunami — your choice

For your chosen scenario, decide: should the hub activate? Assign the initial roles. State the first 5 actions. Explain how decisions and information will flow. This pulls together everything from the course.

Key takeaways

  • Leadership is facilitation, not command.
  • Start simple and build structure as people arrive.
  • Use local information and local resources first.
  • Keep decisions shared, recorded, and visible.
  • Support people — stress, dignity, and wellbeing matter.
Community is not a backup plan. Community is part of the response.
