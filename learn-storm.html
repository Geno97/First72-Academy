Property Damage Mitigation & Storm Response

Limit storm damage to property over three days — preparing before bad weather, staying safe during and after, and recovering without taking dangerous risks.

Draft content. This course is a practical starting point. Please review it and have someone with relevant expertise check anything safety-critical before relying on it.

How this course works. It's built as 3 days — do one module per day (about 25–40 minutes each). Each day has a short video, detailed lessons, a practical activity, and a quick knowledge check. Work at your own pace; your progress is yours to set.

A walkthrough of how storms damage property and the preparation that prevents most of it.

How storms cause damage

Understanding how storms actually damage buildings helps you prepare for the right things. The main culprits are:

  • Wind — lifts roofing, breaks windows, and turns loose objects into dangerous projectiles.
  • Water — driven rain finds gaps; blocked gutters send water into the building.
  • Falling debris — trees, branches, and unsecured items cause much of the worst damage.
  • Power loss — often the longest-lasting disruption, and a hazard in itself.

Securing the outside

Most preventable damage comes from outside the building. Before a storm:

  • Secure or store loose outdoor items — furniture, trampolines, bins, tools. In strong wind these become projectiles.
  • Clear gutters and drains so water flows away rather than backing up into the building.
  • Trim branches near the building and power lines — but never near live lines yourself; that's a job for professionals or the lines company.
  • Check the roof for loose tiles or iron, and secure anything questionable before the wind finds it.
Example
A homeowner left a trampoline un-anchored before a storm. It was thrown into a neighbour's car and fence. A few tie-downs beforehand would have prevented hundreds of dollars of damage and a difficult conversation.

Protecting the inside and your essentials

Inside preparation matters too, especially for the power loss that often follows.

  • Charge phones and any battery packs before the storm hits.
  • Have torches and a battery or hand-crank radio ready — don't rely on candles, which are a fire risk.
  • Know where your water shut-off and switchboard are.
  • Move valuables and electronics away from windows and any areas prone to leaks.
Activity · 15 min

Make your storm prep list

Walk around your property and list everything that would need securing or storing before a storm, plus what you'd need for a likely power cut. Keep the list so it's fast to action next time a warning comes.

How to stay safe during a storm and safely assess your property once it passes.

Staying safe during the storm

Once a storm is underway, the priority is simple: keep people safe. Damage to property can be dealt with later.

  • Stay inside, away from windows and glass doors — flying debris is the main injury risk.
  • Move to the most sheltered part of the building if the storm is severe.
  • Don't go outside to secure things once strong wind has arrived; it's not worth the risk.
  • If you lose power, use torches, not candles. Unplug sensitive electronics to protect them from surges when power returns.
During a storm, people come first. No outside task is worth being struck by flying debris.

The first hours after

When the storm passes, don't rush outside. The aftermath holds its own dangers.

  • Check everyone is safe and uninjured first.
  • Watch for hazards before moving around: downed power lines, broken glass, unstable trees, and damaged structures.
  • Treat every downed power line as live and lethal — stay well back and keep others away.
  • Only then assess your property's damage from a safe distance.

Limiting further damage

Once it's safe, simple temporary measures stop a damaged building getting worse — but only do what's safe and within your ability.

  • Cover broken windows and roof gaps with tarpaulins or boarding to keep rain out.
  • Move undamaged valuables away from water ingress.
  • Photograph all damage before you start any cleanup or temporary repair — insurers need this.
  • Don't climb onto a storm-damaged roof; leave height work to professionals.
Example
After a storm tore part of a roof, the owner safely tarped it from inside the ceiling space and photographed everything first. The insurance claim went smoothly, and the tarp prevented thousands in further water damage overnight.

How to clean up after a storm safely, and when to call in professionals.

Safe clean-up

More people are hurt cleaning up after storms than during them. The work feels urgent, but rushing causes injuries.

  • Wear gloves, sturdy footwear, and eye protection — debris is sharp and unpredictable.
  • Lift carefully and don't overdo it; get help for heavy items rather than straining alone.
  • Keep treating downed lines as live, and report them to your lines company.
  • Be wary of weakened trees and branches under tension — they can move suddenly and violently.

Knowing your limits

The single most important recovery skill is knowing what to leave to professionals. Some jobs simply aren't worth the risk.

  • Leave damaged roofs, large fallen trees, and any line-related hazards to professionals.
  • Don't use a chainsaw on storm-damaged trees unless you're properly trained — they're under unpredictable tension. (First 72 has a separate chainsaw awareness course.)
  • Get an electrician to check any building that's been wet before using the power.
  • If a structure looks unstable, stay out and get it assessed.
Knowing what NOT to take on yourself is the most valuable recovery skill. When in doubt, call a professional.

Recovering well

Recovery is physically and emotionally draining. Looking after people matters as much as fixing buildings.

  • Pace the work — it's a marathon, not a sprint.
  • Stay hydrated and take breaks, especially in cold or wet conditions.
  • Check on neighbours, particularly older people or anyone living alone.
  • Notice stress and fatigue in yourself and others, and talk about it.
  • Keep records and receipts for insurance throughout the recovery.
Example
After a major storm, a neighbourhood organised who would check on whom. They found an elderly resident without power or heating who hadn't called for help. A simple check-in system made the difference.
