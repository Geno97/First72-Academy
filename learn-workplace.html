Workplace Emergency Preparedness

Build a practical, working emergency plan for your worksite over three days — understanding your risks, building your plan, and preparing to look after your people through the first 72 hours.

Draft content. This course is a practical starting point. Please review it and have someone with relevant expertise check anything safety-critical before relying on it.

How this course works. It's built as 3 days — do one module per day (about 25–40 minutes each). Each day has a short video, detailed lessons, a practical activity, and a quick knowledge check. Work at your own pace; your progress is yours to set.

Why preparedness starts with understanding

It's tempting to jump straight to buying a first aid kit or booking a drill. But preparedness that lasts always starts with understanding your specific worksite. A plan copied from another business — or downloaded from a template — rarely fits the realities of your building, your people, and your local hazards.

Think of it like this: two workplaces on the same street can face completely different risks. A ground-floor office with one exit and a riverside location has different priorities to a first-floor studio with no water risk but a single narrow staircase. Only by looking closely at your own site can you build a plan that actually works when it matters.

Scanning your hazards

A hazard is anything that could cause harm. For emergency planning, group them into three types so nothing gets missed:

  • Natural hazards — what's realistic for your area? In New Zealand that often means earthquakes, flooding, severe storms, and in some areas tsunami or volcanic activity. Check your regional council or local Civil Defence group for your area's specific risks.
  • Site-specific hazards — what's particular to your building and work? Chemicals, machinery, heights, vehicle movements, gas, or stored fuel.
  • Knock-on effects — the indirect impacts that disrupt you even without direct damage: loss of power, water, internet, road access, or staff being unable to get to or from work.
Example
A small accounting firm assumed their main risk was fire. When they actually scanned hazards, they realised a single flooded access road would strand staff and cut power for days — a far more likely disruption than fire. That insight reshaped their whole plan toward communication and remote-work backups.

Knowing your people

A plan protects people, so you need a clear picture of who's on site and when. This goes beyond a staff headcount.

  • How many staff, and where they're located across the site through the day.
  • Visitors, clients, and contractors who may be present and won't know your building.
  • Anyone who may need extra help to evacuate — this could be a permanent need (a wheelchair user) or temporary (someone on crutches, or heavily pregnant).
  • Times when the site is most and least populated — your risk profile changes between a busy weekday and a quiet weekend.

You don't need a detailed register of people's medical information — that raises privacy issues. What you need is awareness, and a simple system (like a buddy arrangement) so no one is forgotten in an evacuation.

Setting your priorities

Once you can see your hazards and your people, you can decide what matters most. The universal first priority is always life safety — protecting people from immediate harm. Everything else (property, equipment, business continuity) comes after that.

You can't plan for a worksite you don't understand. Start with a clear, honest picture of your hazards and your people.
Activity · 15 min

Build your worksite risk picture

On one page, write three things: (1) your top three hazards across the three categories above, (2) the consequence of each that would disrupt you most, and (3) a rough note of who's on site at your busiest time and anyone who'd need evacuation help. Keep this — it's the foundation for Days 2 and 3.

Roles and accountability

In an emergency, people freeze if they don't know their role. The solution is to decide responsibilities now, while everyone's calm, and to make sure every key role has a named backup.

  • Emergency wardens lead the evacuation, check areas are clear, and give the all-clear. In larger sites you'll want floor or area wardens too.
  • First aiders — staff with current first aid training who can respond to injuries until help arrives.
  • A communications point — one person who liaises with emergency services so messages are consistent.
  • Alternates for every role — because the person who holds a role won't always be on site when something happens.
Example
A warehouse named one fire warden — who happened to be on leave the week a real evacuation was needed. No one else knew the procedure. Naming two alternates per role would have prevented the confusion that followed.
If a role only has one person, you don't really have that role covered. Always name alternates.

Writing an evacuation plan people actually follow

The best evacuation plan is one people can follow under stress, without stopping to think. That means simple routes, an obvious assembly point, and a reliable way to account for everyone.

  • Mark at least two exit routes from every area, in case one is blocked.
  • Choose an assembly point a safe distance from the building, clear of traffic and falling-hazard zones.
  • Have a method to confirm everyone is out — a printed roll, a sign-in board, or wardens checking and reporting their areas.
  • Plan specifically for visitors and contractors, who won't know your building. Often the simplest answer is that whoever signs them in is responsible for guiding them out.

Walk your routes in person. Plans drawn at a desk often miss the locked fire door, the corridor that's always cluttered, or the assembly point that's actually in the car park's traffic flow.

Communications when the network drops

In a real emergency, phones and internet may fail — through damage, power loss, or simply network overload as everyone tries to call at once. Plan how your team stays in contact when the usual channels are gone.

  • Identify a backup method before you need it — two-way radios, a phone tree, or simply a rule that everyone gathers at the assembly point.
  • Keep a printed contact list; don't rely solely on phones that may be dead or out of reach.
  • Agree who speaks to emergency services and who updates staff, so messages stay consistent.
  • Keep messages short, factual, and calm: say what's known, what's being done, and what people should do next.

Assembling your workplace kit

A workplace emergency kit means you're not scrambling for basics when something happens. Stock it, store it somewhere known and accessible, and check it regularly.

  • First aid supplies, scaled to your team size.
  • Water and some non-perishable food, in case staff are stuck on site.
  • Torches, spare batteries, and a battery or hand-crank radio for official updates.
  • A printed copy of your emergency plan and contact lists.
  • Hi-vis vests for wardens, a basic toolkit, and a first aid manual.
Activity · 20 min

Draft your plan's backbone

Write down: (1) your key roles with two named people each, (2) your two evacuation routes and assembly point, (3) your backup communication method, and (4) where your kit lives and who checks it. This is the working core of your emergency plan.

  • Keep evacuation simple: two routes, a clear assembly point, a headcount method.
  • Agree a backup communication channel before you need it.
  • Stock and regularly check an accessible workplace kit.

How to run an evacuation drill that genuinely improves your plan, and how to lead your team through the first 72 hours.

Why drills matter

A plan on paper isn't enough. Under real stress, people fall back on habit, not instructions they read once. Drills build the habit — they turn your plan into something people can do automatically, and they reveal the gaps you can't see on paper.

Running a drill that sticks

  • Run a full evacuation drill at least once or twice a year — more if you have high staff turnover.
  • Include visitors and contractors where you can, since they're often the most lost in a real event.
  • Time it, and watch closely for where people hesitate, take wrong turns, or bunch up.
  • Debrief honestly afterwards: what worked, what didn't, what surprised you.
  • Update the plan based on what you learned, then test the changes next time.
Example
During a drill, one office discovered their assembly point filled with smoke from a neighbouring building's generator. They'd never have known until a real emergency. They moved the assembly point — a five-minute fix that a drill made obvious.
A drill's real value is the debrief. Finishing fast means nothing; learning something means everything.

The first 72 hours

After a major event — a big earthquake, widespread flooding — emergency services are overwhelmed and prioritise the most life-threatening situations. Your workplace may need to look after its own people for the first crucial days. This is the heart of the 'First 72' idea: be ready to be self-sufficient for roughly three days.

  • Account for everyone and deal with injuries first, using your first aiders and kit.
  • Decide whether to stay or go. Sometimes the safest option is to shelter in place; sometimes it's to leave. Base it on the actual situation and any official advice you can receive.
  • Support people. Shock and stress are normal. Keep people informed, keep them together, and stay calm — your composure sets the tone for everyone.
  • Manage resources. Ration water and supplies sensibly if you might be on site a while.
  • Keep a simple log of decisions and key events — it helps handovers and any later review.
In the first 72 hours, your workplace may be on its own. The preparation from Days 1 and 2 is what carries you through.

Keeping the plan alive

An emergency plan isn't a document you write once and file away. Review it at least yearly, after any drill, and whenever something changes — a renovation, new staff, a new hazard nearby. A living plan protects people; a forgotten one gives false comfort.

Activity · 20 min

Plan your first drill

Set a date for your next evacuation drill. Write down: who'll observe and time it, how you'll include visitors, and three specific things you want to check (e.g. 'does everyone know the assembly point?'). Decide now when you'll debrief afterwards.
