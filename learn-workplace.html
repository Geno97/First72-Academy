Workplace Emergency Preparedness

Build a practical, compliant emergency plan for any worksite — covering hazards, roles, evacuation, communications, kit, drills, and the critical first 72 hours.

Draft content. This course is a practical starting point. Please review it and have someone with relevant expertise check anything safety-critical before relying on it.

Click any module to expand it. Each one has a short lesson, and most include an activity or a quick knowledge check. Work at your own pace.

01

Start here: your risks, your people, your priorities

Know what you're preparing for before you plan.

 OPEN / CLOSE

Good preparedness starts with understanding your own worksite. Before you write any plan, get clear on three things: what could go wrong, who is on site, and what matters most.

Scan your hazards

  • Natural hazards for your area — earthquake, flood, severe weather, fire.
  • Site-specific hazards — chemicals, machinery, height, vehicles, power.
  • Knock-on effects — loss of power, water, access, or communications.

Know your people

  • How many staff, and where they are across the site.
  • Visitors, contractors, and members of the public who may be present.
  • Anyone who may need extra help to evacuate.
You can't plan for a worksite you don't understand. Start with a clear picture.
Activity

Quick risk scan

List your worksite's top three hazards and, for each, the one consequence that would disrupt you most. This becomes the backbone of your plan.

Knowledge check
1. What should come first when building a workplace plan?
Buying equipment Understanding your worksite's hazards and people Running a full evacuation drill
2. Why include knock-on effects in your hazard scan?
Because losing power, water or access can disrupt you even without direct damage They're not important
02

Roles & accountability

Wardens, alternates, and who does what.

 OPEN / CLOSE

In an emergency, people freeze if they don't know their role. Assign clear responsibilities now, and name a backup for every key role in case someone is away or off-site.

Core roles

  • Emergency warden(s) — lead evacuation and check areas are clear.
  • Floor or area marshals — guide people to exits and assembly points.
  • First aiders — trained staff who can respond to injuries.
  • A point of contact — manages communication with emergency services.

Always name alternates

Every key role needs a named backup. The person who holds a role won't always be on site when something happens.

If a role only has one person, you don't really have that role covered.
Knowledge check
1. Why name an alternate for every key role?
So the role is still covered when the main person is away To create more paperwork
2. What does an emergency warden do?
Leads evacuation and checks areas are clear Only calls 111
03

Writing an evacuation plan people follow

Routes, assembly points, and headcounts.

 OPEN / CLOSE

A good evacuation plan is simple enough that people remember it under stress. Map clear routes, name an assembly point everyone knows, and have a way to account for people once they're out.

  • Mark at least two exit routes from every area.
  • Choose an assembly point a safe distance away, clear of the building and traffic.
  • Have a method to confirm everyone is out — a roll, a sign-in board, or area checks.
  • Plan for visitors and contractors who won't know the building.
Activity

Walk your route

Physically walk your primary and backup evacuation routes. Note anything that would slow people down — locked doors, clutter, poor signage — and fix it.

If people can't follow the plan without thinking, it's too complicated.
Knowledge check
1. How many exit routes should each area have?
At least two One is enough
2. Why have a headcount method at the assembly point?
To confirm everyone got out safely It's optional and rarely useful
04

Communications when the network drops

Backup channels and message discipline.

 OPEN / CLOSE

In a real emergency, phones and internet may fail. Plan how your team will stay in contact when the usual channels are down.

  • Identify a backup communication method — two-way radios, a phone tree, or a meeting point.
  • Keep a printed contact list; don't rely only on phones.
  • Agree who speaks to emergency services and who updates staff.
  • Keep messages short, factual, and calm. Say what's known and what's being done.
Decide your backup channel before you need it — not in the middle of the emergency.
Knowledge check
1. Why keep a printed contact list?
Phones and networks may be unavailable Paper looks professional
2. Good emergency messages are:
Short, factual, and calm Long and detailed
05

Assembling the workplace kit

What to stock, and where to keep it.

 OPEN / CLOSE

A workplace emergency kit means you're not scrambling for basics when something happens. Stock it, store it somewhere accessible, and check it regularly.

  • First aid supplies, suited to the size of your team.
  • Water and some non-perishable food for staff who may be stuck on site.
  • Torches, batteries, and a battery or hand-crank radio.
  • A copy of your emergency plan and contact lists.
  • Hi-vis vests for wardens, and a basic toolkit.
Activity

Locate and check

Find your current kit (or decide where one will live). Check expiry dates and that torches work. Set a recurring date to re-check every six months.

Knowledge check
1. Why check the kit regularly?
Supplies expire and batteries go flat It never needs checking
2. Where should the kit be kept?
Somewhere accessible and known to staff Locked away where only one person can reach it
06

Running a drill that sticks

Practising so it works under pressure.

 OPEN / CLOSE

A plan on paper isn't enough. Drills turn it into something people can do without thinking. Run them regularly, then debrief honestly.

  • Run an evacuation drill at least once or twice a year.
  • Include visitors and contractors where you can.
  • Time it, and note where people hesitated or got confused.
  • Debrief afterwards — what worked, what didn't, what to change.
  • Update the plan based on what you learn.
A drill's real value is the debrief. That's where the plan gets better.
Knowledge check
1. How often should you run an evacuation drill?
At least once or twice a year Once, when the plan is written
2. What's the most valuable part of a drill?
The honest debrief that improves the plan Finishing as fast as possible
07

The first 72 hours

Stabilising, supporting people, and coordinating help.

 OPEN / CLOSE

If a major event happens, outside help may be stretched and slow to arrive. Your workplace may need to look after its people for the first crucial days.

  • Account for everyone and address injuries first.
  • Decide whether to stay put or leave, based on the safest option.
  • Support people — stress and shock are normal; keep people informed and calm.
  • Ration supplies sensibly if you may be on site a while.
  • Keep a simple log of decisions and key events.
In the first 72 hours, your workplace may be on its own. Preparation is what carries you through.
Knowledge check
1. Why focus on the first 72 hours?
Outside help may be stretched and slow to arrive Nothing matters after the first hour
2. What's a sensible early action?
Account for everyone and address injuries first Wait silently for instructions
